CoO2 

a cornucopia of wonders

Reduction in dimensionality of a system is one of many ways to create new emergent states and properties, this project is one such attempt to understand the behavior of a 2-dimensional system made up of CoO2. For readers not so familiar with 2-dimensional condensed matter systems, the dimensionality here refers to the number of dimensions in the momentum space of the system (or k-space). An ubiquitous example of 2-dimensional system is graphene, but there are many other exciting systems as even few-layers thick materials are still 2-D systems as their k-space just has 2 dimensions even though in real space they have 3 axis with n-number of layers thickness.

Bulk LiCoO2

Let’s start with understanding LiCoO2, a parent material from which the material science colleagues are trying to synthesis this 2 dimensional CoO2. LiCoO2 is a layered system with alternating layers of Li and CoO2 sheets stacked in A-B-C order. Electronic structure of this system is a simple semiconductor (as expected) with Li giving its one electron off to the CoO2 layer. CoO2 has the Co in an octahedral environment attached to O, which brings in the crystal field splitting to have t2g and eg groups split. Thus sharing the 5 electrons it has with oxygen and the gained 6th electron from Li layers, Co has d-6 configuration with the t2g bands filled and making it a simple gaped system. 

2-Dimensonal CoO2

Having understood bulk LiCoO2, we can make the system interesting by removing the Li off the CoO2 layer, thus depriving the Co of its 6th electron and try to create a unsatisfied Co because of which interesting correlations and band filling can occur. Interesting effects are expected in monolayer CoO2 because of two reasons
 
1) Co in the monolayer CoO2 are placed in a triangular lattice. Since we are forcing the system to be partially filled, spin interactions are going to come into play, which in triangular lattice leads to frustration and spin correlations.
2) Since Co is a d shell system, partially filled Co might have strongly correlated physics dictating the configuration which breaks the individual particle picture.

Figure 6 shows the electronic band structure of the 2 dimensional CoO2 system at various level of theories. Interesting part happens while using QSGW theory to calculate the energy levels. 

Before to dive into it, let’s have a quick idea of what QSGW is. Quasiparticle self consistent GW theory is a parameter free theory derived from the famous Hedin’s equation. This is a theory added upon the GW approximation in the Hedin’s equation. Hedin’s equation is a self-consistent set of equations that can be used to get the eigenvalues of a set of interacting particles given the eigenvalues of non-interacting particles. In GW approximation we limit the number of Feynman diagrams coming into the calculation of self energy to first order when expanded in perturbation of W (the screened Coulomb interaction). This can also be thought of as setting the Gamma* diagrams in the Figure 17 to 0 while converting the bare coulomb interaction to screened/dressed interaction or setting Gamma in the first feynman diagram to 1. For further reading you can go through this not-so-deep article written by me.

Having introduced GW, and knowing that it is a parameter free theory, interesting things can be seen in the figure. Calculating the QSGW bands with non interacting Hamiltonian as LDA and LDA+U ideally should give us the same self energy and thus the same final calculated bands, but interestingly, for monolayer CoO2 starting from LDA and LDA+U gives metallic and insulating band structures respectively as shown. Which would indicate the system has two set of solutions (or at least local minimas) for its self energy. Further analysis is required to understand which exactly is the lowest energy minimum in self energy. Since this involves many body theory, the definition and calculation of energy is not exactly trivial and often times RPA (random phase approximation) level energies are used. One needs to think a bit more as RPA level diagrams shown in figure might not just be sufficient for the resulting two phase separation in self energy space.

2-Dimensonal LiCoO2

Since our experimental friends are trying to realize the 2 dimensional CoO2 using bulk LiCoO2, we started looking at the effects of 2-D CoO2’s close cousin 2-D LiCoO2. As we saw in the bulk case, one would expect similar physics to dictate the quantum mechanics of monolayer LiCoO2. But to our surprise we saw that within LDA approximation, monolayer LiCoO2 is metallic (half metal, to be precise) while its bulk from is gaped semiconductor.  

Upon closer inspection, we see that there seems to be an extra band that pops in at the fermi level as shown in figure. Curious as to where this band came from, we calculated the wave function of that band at gamma point and visualized it, surprisingly this belongs to a state that eerily looks like 2-dimensional electron gas, furthermore there seems to be spin polarization.

This video shows the quantum band structure of LiCoO2 monolayer when Li is moved away from the CoO2 layer. One can see the Nearly free electron like bands forming more rigidly as the Li is seperated from the CoO2. This shows that the 2 dimensional electron gas like band is actually formed by Li-Li interaction in its elemental state.
