CoO2 

a cornucopia of wonders

Reduction in dimensionality of a system is one of many ways to create new emergent states and properties, this project is one such attempt to understand the behavior of a 2-dimensional system made up of CoO2. For readers who are not familiar with 2-dimensional condensed matter systems, the dimensionality here refers to the number of dimensions in the momentum space of the system (or k-space). An ubiquitous example of 2-dimensional system is graphene, there are many more exciting systems, even systems that are few-layers thick. These few layer thick materials are still 2-D systems as their k-space has only 2 dimensions even though in real space they have 3 axis with n-number of layers giving them thickness.

Bulk LiCoO2

Let’s start with understanding LiCoO2, a parent material from which the material science colleagues are trying to synthesis this 2 dimensional CoO2. LiCoO2 is a layered system with alternating layers of Li and CoO2 sheets stacked in A-B-C order. Electronic structure of this system is a simple semiconductor (as expected) with Li giving its one electron off to the CoO2 layer. CoO2 has the Co in an octahedral environment attached to O, which brings in the crystal field splitting to have t2g and eg groups split. Thus with the 5 electrons it shares with oxygen and the gained 6th electron from Li layers, Co has d-6 configuration with the t2g bands filled which makes it a simple gaped system. 

2-Dimensonal CoO2

Having understood bulk LiCoO2, we can make the system interesting by removing the Li off the CoO2 layer which deprives Co of its 6th electron. This would make the Co unsatisfied because of which interesting correlations and band filling can occur. Interesting effects are expected in monolayer CoO2 because of two reasons
 
1) Co in the monolayer CoO2 are placed in a triangular lattice. Since we are forcing the system to be partially filled, spin interactions are going to come into play, which in triangular lattice leads to frustration and spin correlations.
2) Since Co is a d shell system, partially filled Co might have strongly correlated physics dictating the configuration which breaks the individual particle picture.

Figure 6 shows the electronic band structure of the 2 dimensional CoO2 system at various level of theories. Interesting part happens while using QSGW theory to calculate the energy levels. 

Before we dive into it, let’s have a quick idea of what QSGW is. Quasiparticle self consistent GW theory is a parameter free theory derived from the famous Hedin’s equation. This is a theory added upon the GW approximation in the Hedin’s equation. Hedin’s equation is a self-consistent set of equations that can be used to get the eigenvalues of a set of interacting particles given the eigenvalues of non-interacting particles. In GW approximation we limit the number of Feynman diagrams coming into the calculation of self energy to first order when expanded in perturbation of W (the screened Coulomb interaction). This can also be thought of as setting the Gamma* diagrams in the Figure 17 to 0 while converting the bare coulomb interaction to screened/dressed interaction or setting Gamma in the first feynman diagram to 1. For further reading you can go through this not-so-deep article written by me.

Having introduced GW, and knowing that it is a parameter free theory, interesting things can be seen in the band structure figure. Calculating the QSGW bands with non interacting Hamiltonian as LDA and LDA+U ideally should give us the same self energy (as the theory is parameter free and should not be influensed, atleast ideally, by the starting point) and thus the same final calculated bands, but interestingly, for monolayer CoO2 starting from LDA and LDA+U gives metallic and insulating band structures respectively as shown. Which would indicate the system has two set of solutions (or at least local minimas) for its self energy. Further analysis is required to understand which exactly is the lowest energy minimum in self energy. Since this involves many body theory, the definition and calculation of total energy of systems is not exactly trivial and often times RPA (random phase approximation) level energies are used. One needs to think a bit more as RPA level diagrams shown in figure might not just be sufficient for the resulting two phase separation in self energy space.

2-Dimensonal LiCoO2

Since our experimental friends are trying to realize the 2 dimensional CoO2 using bulk LiCoO2, we started looking at the effects of 2-D CoO2’s close cousin 2-D LiCoO2. As we saw in the bulk case, one would expect similar physics to dictate the quantum mechanics of monolayer LiCoO2. But to our surprise we saw that within LDA approximation, monolayer LiCoO2 is metallic (half metal, to be precise) while its bulk from is gaped semiconductor.  

Upon closer inspection, we see that there seems to be an extra band that pops in at the fermi level as shown in figure. Curious as to where this band came from, we calculated the wave function of that band at gamma point and visualized it, surprisingly this belongs to a state that eerily looks like 2-dimensional electron gas (2DEG) , furthermore there seems to be spin polarization. Further, calculating the number of electrons in the 2DEG, we see that there is about 0.24 electrons in the 2DEG and about 0.35 electrons near Li. 

This seems strange because what this tells us is that monolayer LiCoO2 would have a quantum mechanical ground state which would rather than have an electron from Li given to the CoO2 layer, just give 0.75 of its electron and have the rest to iteself forming the 2DEG like state. 

Before proceeding to understand the origins and mechanism of formtion of this electron gas, lets look at few amazing things we can do with this system. Figure shows the formation of 1-Dimensional electron gas along rows of lithium placed on top of CoO2. This is interesting because  it is extremely hard to experimentally create a stable one dimensional electron gas.

Since we now know that there is a nascent electron state forming above Li whenever it is placed above CoO2 layer, we can even observe a first of its kind, 0-dimensional electron or a quantum dot state of electron gas when we place a single Li ontop of a big 2-D sheet of CoO2 as shown bellow

Current work is to understand the system better. Ideally one would want to come up with a mechanism to write down the Hamiltonian explaining the physics behind this. There has been a series of tests done to probe the system for different clues to understand whats exactly happening. There are some intriguing puzzles to be solved, for one, somehow whenever the Li is covered by CoO2 layers in bottom and top, it prefers to give away a full electron, while anisotropy in the Li’s z axis it prefers to have 0.25 of the electron to itself. 

Let us do a computational experiment ( or as theorists would say, thought experiment, but in a computer) let us pull the Li in LiCoO2 monolayer away slowly. At one point, when we pull the Li far enough, one should get an identity of CoO2 monolayer forming in the band structure. Here is the band structure of the Li being pulled away slowly. 

One can immediately see some interesting things happening. When Li is the farthest, instead of getting back the LDA like band structure of monolayer CoO2 shown in figure, we see that the the free electron like band that is responsible for the 2DEG is forming more “rigidly”. This tells us that these bands actually belong to pure Li forming states with each other in the neighboring cell. The power of theorists is that, we can test this hypothesis even though thats totally an unphysical system. 
Figure above shows the band structure of elemental Li in the same unit cell. It is basically LiCoO2 with the CoO2 layer removed and now we clearly see the 2DEG state that is nothing but Li-Li tight binding interaction with both Li-s and Li-p orbital states forming bonding wave functions as shown in the color picture which has the band structure projected onto the respective orbitals. Again, the plot of electron density of this shows 2DEG forming. One thing to note is that it is the Li-s states (red ones in the figure) is the one thats mostly occupied (bellow fermi level) even in the elemental Li. And also that the s-p hybrid states are not that high in energy and this might be closely related to the fact that we can see the 2DEG in our state has hints of pz orbital character in it from the electron density pictures in the earlier figures.  
Projecting the initial LiCoO2 monolayer on Li states actually tells at a different story. The figure above shows the comparison of pure elemental Li and LiCoO2. As seen the occupied in elemental Li and LiCoO2 are actually different, in LiCoO2, its the Li-p states that’s producing the 2DEG and lies near the fermi. This result still needs to be understood better but is none the less one clue taking us closer to the big answer.
